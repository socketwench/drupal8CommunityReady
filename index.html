Drupal at a Crossroads

In many ways, Drupal today is at a kind of crossroads. There's been a lot of speculation, hope, excitement, even a little fear. Developers and businesses alike have a lot to be both concerned, and excited about.

Huge Changes

That we really needed

There's been some huge changes with over the last several years. Critics have called Drupal 8 a completely different CMS from the previous version. While intimidating, there were many changes we needed for Drupal to be ready for the now and the future.

Gigantic Dev Cycle

Four years!

There's also been a gigantic development cycle for this version, longer than any other we've done in the history of the project. Four years! That's an eternity in the tech world.

"When it's Ready"

Led to speculation, worry

We also had the nagging problem that we never really knew when Drupal 8 was going to be released. We said, "When it's ready". This created a lot of speculation and even worry that Drupal would never get out the door.

Community Shakeup

Long time devs left or reduced involvement

While others joined the project

We've also had some long time developers leave the project, or reduce their involvement. From an outsider perspective it's hard to read this in an alarming way, forgetting about the many new developers that have joined the project recently and had a big impact.

Forked!

Not the first, but highest profile

And let's not forget the big fork Drupal had recently. It wasn't the first, but it was certainly the highest profile.

"It's too different!"

But change can be good

A lot of this seems like it's all just too much. That Drupal 8 feels too different. Yet, change can be a very, very good thing.

But let me tell you a different story...

Let's go back to 2012

Drupal 8 had just gotten started

And I was sitting in an auditorium listening to Larry Garfield give a talk an "Musical CMS: How Symfony is coming to Drupal".
This was my second Drupal camp. I actually had given a talk just the previous day on how does Drupal 7 work. See, for year's I had used Drupal for my own site, but I never really developed any code for it, nor peaked inside core. I decided to change that. I set up a debugger and stepped through the entire Drupal 7 init process. And that's when I discovered something kinda surprising.

Drupal 7 is weird

Drupal 7 is really, really weird. While I had largely taught myself coding, I had done so in C++. From there I learned several other languages then finally PHP. And D7 didn't look like anything I had come to expect.

Anachronisms

Straight functions (mostly)

PHP4's OOP was odd & incomplete

Drupal 7 is, to me, full of anachronisms. It's composed of straight functions -- mostly. There isn't anything wrong with that, it only feels...well...old fashioned. It was also justified in Drupal's case as PHP 4's OOP support was odd and incomplete.

Hooks are API Go-Fish

Do you have a _____ hook?

The thing that really bothered me was hooks, hooks are like API Go-fish. I had been coding for over a decade at that point. I've been through 4 years of college, worked in multiple languages and products, and I had never saw anything like hooks. Drupal 7, in essence, asks each module "Do you have a something-hook?" and if it does, it executes it. I just couldn't believe the project I had come to love the last few years works like that. I was dumbfounded.

Code for everything!

Even for things that shouldn't be code

That wasn't the only thing that bothered me. Drupal used code for everything. Menu items, forms, and tons of other things that shouldn't be code. I did't understand why we didn't save these in flat-files like many, many other projects.

Arrays of doom!

Undocumentable monstrosities

With the exception of the database layer, there were huge arrays of doom everywhere in Drupal. Either you needed to create them in your code, or work with huge, undocumentable monstrosities that you needed to run through a debugger first BEFORE writing your code. WTH!?
Drupal 7 was also built with the assumption of the desktop web at its core. When a request comes in, Drupal processes it, routes it to the appropriate handlers, and then returns HTML for display. With frameworks like React and mobile applications, the need to return HTML has gotten smaller and smaller.

Bootstrapping Drupal is slow!

Everything is loaded even when not needed

drupal_bootstrap() is also really slow! Even if Drupal 7 was returning a REST response, it would still load the theme layer, load up all modules, and require a lot of CPU and memory resources. This makes Drupal 7 unattractive as a backend to decoupled and native applications.

Not-Invented-Here (NIH)

PHP4 dependencies were copy-paste

Drupal 7 didn't make this easier

Drupal also has a bad habit of developing their own solutions rather than working with others'. This is the "Not Invented Here" problem. In earlier versions of PHP, there was a good reason for this as including external dependencies was laborious. We literally had to copy and paste code into our repository. Today, however, there are many excellent libaries and tools we could leverage, yet Drupal 7 did not provide any natural way to do so.

NIH has a social cost

In-community preferred despite costs

Sunk-cost fallacy makes replacement impossible

Not-invented-here also has a big social cost. It creates a culture where in-community solutions are immediately preferred even in the face of time, stress, and maintainership costs. Once in place, NIH motivated code can become impossible to replace due to the sunk-cost fallacy.

Sunk-cost Fallacy

"Point of no return" thinking

Works in software design too

Haven't heard of the Sunk-cost Fallacy? It's an idea from behavioral economics that suggests once we've invested a certain amount of utility in something, we pass a point of "no return" where we're obligated to stick with it even when we know the problems. Usually this refers to cars, houses, movie or concert tickets, but it works in software design as well! Often, it's a key motivator behind forks.
After discovering all of this, I was really discouraged. The coding techniques I had been familiar with for years just didn't apply to Drupal. It all seemed too strange, too weird, and too impenetrable for me to even seriously consider writing a module or contributing to core.

I <3 Drupal

Just don't tell me how you work

I really, really love you Drupal, but please, don't tell me how you work.
The thing was, I had assumed I was alone. That no one else had this bizarre perspective on our beloved CMS. That is, until I was sitting in that auditorium listening to Crell talk about putting Symfony in Drupal. And then I got really, really excited.

One of the benefits of modern OOP is that it reduces "Drupalisms", of which hooks was one of the biggest offenders. These project- specific patterns have long been a barrier to developers that would otherwise look to other CMSes. OOP and reduced Drupalisms benefits longtime Drupal developers too. Drupal 8 provides a familiar environment to develop a broader base of skills.

So what didn't we do so well?

As much as I'm proud and excited about Drupal 8, there's a lot of things I feel we should work on.

There are also Technical Things

There are always technical things we could have done better. We could have chosen a different routing system, a different front end...and while those things are important, they're the least important things we should improve.

FUD

Fear Uncertainty and Doubt

One of the first things I think we could do better in the future, and I admit, also a personal axe to grind, is FUD. Fear Uncertainty, and Doubt.

FUD easily created Unintentionally

Social media echo chamber

FUD is easily created unintentionally, especially in Open Source. With so many voices from so many backgrounds it's easy for any irregularity, difference of opinion, or unexpected delay to result in fear, uncertainty and doubt. This is amplified by the social media echo chamber where concerns can be reposted spread virally in seconds.

Drupal 8 Created a lot of FUD

Drupal 8 created a lot of FUD. Any popular project that undergoes a big refactoring as Drupal did inevitability results in FUD. Our seemingly never-ending development cycle helped little, but that wasn't all.

People Turnover

When a long-time project member leaves...

...it can be seen as a sign the project is failing.

Drupal 8 had several people either leaving the project entirely or taking on reduced roles. When this happens in the middle of a dev cycle, it can be seen as a sign the project is failing.

Other Missteps

"Secret" sprints & "heads down" behavior

We've also had some other missteps that created FUD. Sequestering core developers for dedicated, private sprinting and other "heads down" behavior may be forgotten now, but it was a serious worry that created a needless information vacuum in which FUD can flourish.

Fighting FUD

Needs clear, and consistent communication

So how can we fight FUD in when we start working on the next version of Drupal? We need clear, and consistent communication.

Not easy in OSS

For smaller projects, the lead dev or original author

Larger projects, sponsoring corporation or foundation

This isn't easy in open source! Often it requires a single point of communication. In smaller projects this is often the lead developer or original author. With a project as large as Drupal, it usually falls to the sponsoring corporation, foundation, or association.

Not an easy Job

And it is a job.

"Contrib Champion", "Core Ombudsman"

This isn't an easy job either, and it is a job. Providing clear and consistent communication throughout a development cycle is demanding. If it doesn't come from an authoritative source, it won't have clout to fight fear, uncertainty and doubt. I like calling this role the "Contrib Champion" others call it a "Core Ombudsman". No matter what they're called, I think it's an essential piece for Drupal's future success.

BURNOUT

For four years we worked on Drupal 8. While I didn't spend a lot of time with core, I did put a lot of effort into developing one of the first Drupal 8 modules. And I have to tell you, it was exhausting. Today more than one developer in Drupal is suffering from a psychological and physiological condition known as burnout.

What causes burnout

The candle the burns twice as bright burns half as long.
While I can go into a long description of burnout and what causes it, I find the following quote a great summary. 'The candle that burns twice as bright burns half as long.' This is from the film Blade Runner. Like many coders, we in Drupal often enjoy working on Drupal both as a day job and as something fun to do. More than one developer working on Drupal 8 was caught up in this pattern. For a short period of time, this is okay, but four years is a very, very long time.

Reducing Your Burnout Risk

Do...less.

A few developers I've spoken to have suffered burnout during Drupal 8's development cycle. Solutions are often very personal, but some patterns exist. We can reduce the risk of burning out by...doing less. This seems obvious, but it can be very, very difficult to do. We're passionate in the Drupal community, but we can't subsist on passion alone.

Doing Less for Yourself

Seek out a co-maintainer

If your a module dev or contribute in your free time, take a break. Seek out a co-maintainer to keep things moving while you're away.
